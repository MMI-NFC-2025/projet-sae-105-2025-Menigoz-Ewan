The Researcher

Mary Anning: A Life Devoted to Earth's Secrets

A Childhood on the Coast

Mary Anning was born on May 21, 1799, in Lyme Regis, a small coastal town in England. She was one of ten children of Richard and Mary Anning, but only Mary and her older brother Joseph survived infancy. The family was modest; her father supplemented his income by collecting and selling "curiosities"—fossils found on the local cliffs—to tourists.

At 15 months old, she was struck by lightning while being held by a neighbor who was killed in the storm. Mary was the only one to survive.

From an early age, she learned to spot fossils revealed by tides and landslides—a dangerous job because the cliffs were unstable.

Her father died in 1810 when she was only 11. The Anning family faced even greater poverty. Mary and her brother Joseph continued to search for fossils and sell them to support their mother.

In 1811 Joseph discovered a skull of an unknown marine creature. A few months later, Mary, then 12, found the rest of the skeleton. It was the first complete ichthyosaur skeleton ever discovered—a marine reptile that would upend understanding of prehistoric life.

An Adulthood Filled with Discoveries

Growing up, Mary became an expert on the cliffs of Lyme Regis.

In 1823, she discovered the first complete plesiosaur skeleton.

In 1828, she discovered the first pterodactyl fossil found outside Germany (Dimorphodon).

She was also the first to note that the strange stones she called "bezoar stones" were in fact coprolites—fossilized feces—offering new insight into prehistoric diets.

Despite her brilliance, Mary's life was often difficult.

Fossil hunting was physically demanding and risky.

She had a faithful companion, her dog Tray, who always accompanied her on perilous explorations. Tragically, in 1833, while she was searching for fossils, a landslide swept Tray away, killing him instantly. Mary survived but was deeply affected by his loss.

Although her discoveries enriched many museums and private collections, she struggled for broad recognition and financial security.

Mary Anning died of breast cancer on March 9, 1847, at age 47.

Her life was an unrelenting quest for Earth's buried secrets, leaving a monumental legacy that laid the foundations of modern paleontology and inspired generations of scientists.

Impact and Legacy

Mary Anning's discoveries played a major role in the evolution of paleontology and contributed to debates on evolution and extinction of species.

A victim of the Matilda Effect, her work was often attributed to male scientists who bought her fossils and published papers without mentioning her name.

Through her discoveries, Mary Anning provided irrefutable evidence of the existence of extinct species.

This contradicted the prevailing religious belief that no species could disappear.

By demonstrating that entire life forms once existed but are now extinct, Mary Anning not only validated the notion of extinction but also supported the idea of a timescale far greater than that suggested by religious texts.
