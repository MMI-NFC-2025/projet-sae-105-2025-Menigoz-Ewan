Scientific and historical terms related to Mary Anning and her discoveries.

Fossilized feces. Mary Anning was one of the first to correctly identify these fossils and understand their scientific value.
Section of the Dorset and East Devon coast, UNESCO World Heritage site, rich in fossils from the Jurassic period.
Fossil genus of pterosaurs, belonging to the Dimorphodontidae family and living in the Early Jurassic.
Systematic denial or minimization of women scientists' contributions to research, often attributed to men, named after Matilda Gage.
Complete disappearance of a species. Mary Anning's discoveries demonstrated that extinction was a real phenomenon.
Prehistoric marine reptile resembling a dolphin, lived during the Mesozoic. Mary Anning discovered the first complete skeleton in 1811.
Geological period spanning from -201 to -145 million years, during which many species discovered by Mary Anning lived.
Coastal town in Dorset, England, birthplace of Mary Anning and site of her major discoveries.
Science that studies living beings that populated the Earth during geological times, based on the study of fossils.
Prehistoric marine reptile with a long neck and four flippers, which lived during the Mesozoic. Discovered by Mary Anning in 1823.

It is a relatively small and late type of Pterosaur from the Jurassic I. It had long wings and a very long skull and a very short tail. Often incorrectly used by the general public as a synonym for all Pterosaurs.
