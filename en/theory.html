Popularization of Her Theory

Although Mary Anning did not develop a theory per se, her discoveries greatly contributed to the understanding of evolution and extinction of species, thus supporting Charles Darwin's theory of evolution.

Key Concepts Revealed

Extinction of Species

The discoveries of fossil reptiles proved that entire species could disappear, a controversial concept since it was believed God had created all existing species. The skeletons found were so radically different from current creatures that the idea of extinction became irrefutable.

Age of Species

The fossils she discovered helped demonstrate that the Earth was much older than previously believed.

Coprolites

She identified these "stones" as fossilized feces, opening a window onto prehistoric diets. They support the theory of evolution and the fact that species can indeed go extinct.

Impact on Modern Science

With her meticulous observations and intuitive understanding of anatomy, Mary Anning influenced scientists such as Charles Lyell and Richard Owen.

Although she never published scientific articles, her expertise was recognized and consulted by the greatest scientists of her time.

Late Recognition

In 2010, the Royal Society included Mary Anning among the ten British women who had the greatest influence on the history of science.

Today, her legacy inspires a new generation of paleontologists and advocates for equality in the sciences.
